LoRaWAN Network Overview

Network Overview

Selecting the right network technology is crucial for effective agricultural IoT deployments. Various options exist, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. The table below summarizes key network types, highlighting their advantages and disadvantages in the context of smart farming applications.

Name Description Advantages Disadvantages
Ethernet Wired local network connection using twisted‑pair or fibre cables.
  • High reliability and speed (100 Mbps–1 Gbps+)
  • Low latency and low interference
  • Secure and stable
  • Requires cabling — costly and impractical across large fields
  • Limited mobility
  • Vulnerable to cable damage from weather or machinery
Wi‑Fi Wireless LAN based on IEEE 802.11 for short–medium range links (~100 m).
  • High data rates (tens to hundreds of Mbps)
  • Easy integration with consumer devices
  • Low cost hardware
  • High power consumption
  • Limited range and coverage in open farms
  • Susceptible to interference and dropouts
Cellular (4G / 5G) Public mobile networks offering wide‑area connectivity via licensed spectrum.
  • Very wide coverage through carriers
  • High throughput and reliability (where available)
  • Enables direct cloud connectivity
  • Recurring data‑plan costs
  • Moderate–high power usage
  • Coverage may be poor in remote rural areas
NB‑IoT (Narrowband IoT) Cellular LPWAN using narrowband channels within licensed spectrum (LTE family).
  • Excellent penetration (indoor/underground)
  • Long range (up to ~10 km)
  • Very low power consumption
  • Operator support
  • Recurring data‑plan costs
  • Low data rates (kbps)
  • Dependent on carrier deployment
  • Higher latency for some use cases
Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) Short‑range personal area network in 2.4 GHz for low‑power devices.
  • Very low power consumption
  • Widely supported by smartphones
  • Good for wearable/handheld sensors
  • Very short range (~10–50 m)
  • Limited device count and topology
  • Requires a gateway for long‑range backhaul
Zigbee / Z‑Wave Mesh networking protocols for low‑power sensors (Zigbee = 2.4 GHz, Z‑Wave ≈ 900 MHz).
  • Low power and self‑healing mesh
  • Mature ecosystem for home/localized monitoring
  • Good for greenhouses, barns, small clusters
  • Short‑to‑medium range per hop (<100 m)
  • Limited bandwidth
  • Complex setup and management for large deployments
Satellite Communication via LEO/GEO satellites for global coverage.
  • Works anywhere on Earth
  • No reliance on local infrastructure
  • Useful for extremely remote or mobile assets
  • High hardware and airtime costs
  • High latency
  • Typically higher power requirements
LoRaWAN Long‑range, low‑power wide‑area network using unlicensed sub‑GHz spectrum.
  • Very low power and long range (2–15 km typical)
  • Open standard and low‑cost gateways
  • Ideal for sparse, battery‑powered sensors
  • Low data rate and limited payload size
  • Duty‑cycle and regional regulatory restrictions
  • Requires gateway coverage or local infrastructure
